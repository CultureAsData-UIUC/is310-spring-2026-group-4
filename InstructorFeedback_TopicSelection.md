I wanted to highlight a few key themes that emerged across your individual projects, which I think will help you all as you move forward with your analyses, as well as some suggestions on additional, relevant scholarship:

1. **Food as Cultural Class Markers**

While your focus on digital food culture is strong, I also think almost all of you in different ways are also approaching this as a topic of cultural class construction. And so I would encourage you to think about how you might pull out that theme more explicitly in your shared principles. Generally, though I want to again commend you for your thoughtful approach to food as culturally produced, so hopefully this is just a nudge to think more about the material realities that undergird the cultural representations you're analyzing.

2. **Platform Politics & Internet Ephemerality**

A number of you are using contemporary platforms, which makes sense but there is a conceptual risk in this topic is treating TikTok, Instagram, and other platforms and most of you seem to see these not as neutral venues for food culture, which is excellent. I would highly encourage all of you to think both about how ranking algorithms and platform affordances might shape your data collection, but also how you will preserve your data. The content you find today might not be there tomorrow. Furthermore, as we will discuss a bit more in class, archived web pages like those in the Internet Archive are actually composite representations of multiple snapshots, so generally you should see these less as stable sources and more as artifacts of a particular moment in time that may not be fully representative of the content that was originally there. I think generally though you are all thinking carefully about the digital infrastructures shaping food culture, so well done on that front!

Your overall topic sits at the intersection of food studies, media studies, platform studies, critical race studies, and information science. There is substantial scholarship examining food as culture, digital food representation, and the politics of culinary authenticity.

By positioning "Western" as the default and everything else as "ethnic," you're implicitly centering Western food as unmarked and normal while marking all other cuisines as Other. This reproduces exactly the kind of bias you're trying to study. What do these categories actually mean? Is "Western" European and North American? Does it include Latin America? Australia? Is "ethnic" a synonym for non-Western? For Asian? For immigrant cuisines in Western countries? These are not self-evident categories—–they're politically loaded terms that need careful unpacking. "Ethnic cuisine" is also a somewhat outdated catch-all term that flattens enormous diversity. Chinese, Indian, Ethiopian, and Mexican cuisines are wildly different from each other, but all get lumped together as "ethnic" in contrast to an unmarked "Western" default. I would encourage you to think much more carefully about what you're actually trying to compare and why.

Some questions to consider:

- Are you defining these categories based on how creators describe their own content?
- Are you looking at how platforms categorize cuisine (if they do)?
- Are you examining viewer perceptions of what counts as "ethnic" versus "normal" food?
- Or are you imposing these categories as an analytical framework?

Each of these would be a different project with different implications. You might find it more productive to focus on specific cuisines or culinary traditions (Korean, Italian, West African, etc.) and examine how they're represented and engaged with on TikTok, rather than trying to use the blunt categories of "Western" and "ethnic."

Even if you refine your categories, you'll need to think carefully about the relationship between cuisine and nationality, ethnicity, and cultural community. These are complex, contested relationships. Who gets to claim what food? Is "Italian food" defined by geography (made in Italy), by ethnicity (made by Italian people), by technique (using Italian methods), or by ingredients? What about Italian-American food, or Italian fusion? There's a politics to food ownership and authenticity that you need to engage with, not avoid. It might actually be interesting to examine how cooking profiles themselves deploy nationality or cultural identity. Do creators explicitly claim cultural authority ("authentic [insert ethnicity] home cooking"), or do they avoid those labels? This could reveal something about how food and identity are negotiated on platforms.

Right now, your topics of interest are very broad: cooking profiles, trending hashtags, what cuisine gets the most engagement. This doesn't yet constitute a clear research question. What are you actually trying to understand? Another possible direction for you to explore might be focusing on what cooking content goes viral most consistently on TikTok, then examine whether there are patterns in terms of cuisine type, presentation style, or creator identity. This would let you investigate whether certain cuisines are systematically favored or marginalized by the platform's algorithm and audience engagement patterns, without presupposing what those patterns are. Another direction: examine how specific cuisines are framed when they do go viral. Are they presented as "exotic" and "authentic," or as accessible and easy?
