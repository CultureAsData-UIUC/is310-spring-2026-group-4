# Instructor Feedback on Initial Dataset Submission

Overall, this initial dataset is a solid submission with a genuinely interesting focus. Choosing to study everyday constraint-driven food practices among college students is smart precisely because it's a topic you have direct experience with. That proximity gives you good instincts about what counts as realistic versus idealized content, and it shows throughout. The use of two platforms is also well-motivated, and I appreciate that you articulate clearly what each one offers: TikTok for visual presentation, Reddit for how people describe and narrate their situations. That's a meaningful methodological distinction that will serve you well as you develop the analysis.

**1. What's Working and Where to Push Further**

I like the dataset structure section, though I'd recommend converting it into a proper markdown table with data types and at least one concrete example entry; that would make it much more legible to outside readers. It's also worth renaming the spreadsheet to remove the "Sheet1" artifact from Google Sheets before sharing it more widely.

The transparency about your computational tools is appreciated, as is your honest acknowledgment of platform constraints and algorithmic opacity. Those are real methodological issues worth keeping visible in your documentation, as well as your final data eassy. I also appreciate that you limited the dataset to public posts and developed a clear methodology for selection. The decision to require some contextual framing and to exclude highly aestheticized content is smart and well-reasoned. One suggestion for your final data essay: include a concrete example of one of those "perfect" videos you excluded, to help readers understand where you drew the line and why.

I appreciate your honesty in the challenges section. Your points about assessing implied constraints, interpreting tone, and the difficulty of comparing across two very different platforms all make sense, and the consistency challenge over time is real. I'd encourage you to use your final data essay to detail how you actually refined your categories during collection (what changed, what edge cases pushed you to rethink a label) since that's exactly the kind of interpretive process the assignment wants you to document.

On the observations: the patterns you identify around speed, convenience, and common strategies are largely what you would expect given your focus and selection methodology. The final observation––that TikTok content is more visually polished while Reddit is more direct and detailed, often including advice-seeking and substantive responses––is the most interesting one, and I would encourage you to pull on that thread more. Digging into how content is framed differently across the two platforms, and what type of information gets shared in each context, might actually be easier to compare than the food practices themselves and would give your analysis more traction.

**2. Next Steps and Scaling**

I would gently push back on the framing of "simple computational methods" throughout the next steps section; it undersells the complexity of what you're actually proposing. A few more specific suggestions: for Reddit, rather than scraping from scratch, you might look into existing archived datasets via Pushshift, which has collected Reddit data since at scale and would save you significant technical overhead. You can read more about it here Baumgartner, Jason, Savvas Zannettou, Brian Keegan, Megan Squire, and Jeremy Blackburn. “The Pushshift Reddit Dataset.” *Proceedings of the International AAAI Conference on Web and Social Media* 14 (May 2020): 830–39. [https://doi.org/10.1609/icwsm.v14i1.7347](https://doi.org/10.1609/icwsm.v14i1.7347), and I would be happy to help you get the datasets locally.

For TikTok, scraping is going to be tricky due to platform constraints, so you'll likely need to continue collecting manually. One thing I mentioned previously is that you should  download and locally archive the videos themselves rather than just saving URLs, since TikTok content disappears regularly. If you do want to explore scraping, I would recommend looking at PykTok [https://github.com/dfreelon/pyktok](https://github.com/dfreelon/pyktok), though generally TikTok tries to block scrapers.

One thing I would strongly recommend adding to your dataset is a field recording how you found each post, that is the specific search query or hashtag that surfaced it. That is a meaningful data point in itself, and it will also help you account for and mitigate algorithmic influence on your sample as you scale up. For the text analysis side, you might take a look at the Python library `shorttext` [https://pypi.org/project/shorttext/](https://pypi.org/project/shorttext/), which is specifically optimized for shorter texts like social media comments and captions. It might be worth trying it out on your existing dataset first to see if it can help you identify patterns that inform your keyword-based labeling rules before you expand. I especially like your idea of comparing computer-generated labels against your manual ones. That comparison could be really illuminating about where automated approaches work and where they break down for this kind of interpretive work. One thing also worth keeping in mind: your Reddit posts go further back in time than your TikTok content, and it's worth thinking about whether that temporal difference skews the content in ways that matter for your analysis.

Finally, as you think about scaling, I'd encourage you to think about scale not just as expanding the size of the dataset but also the depth of your annotations. You might end up finding it more interesting to experiment with the text analysis or even doing multimodal analysis rather than simply expanding your analysis. That approach might be an interesting complement to your most interesting interpretive work, which is the constraint and tone analysis. And as you noted, those are exactly the fields most at risk of getting flattened as the dataset grows. Another option might be developing command-line validation script, which could help you maintain consistency, and keeping the richer interpretive labeling intact should be a priority even as you automate other parts of the process.

Overall this is a good foundation and the topic has real cultural depth. Happy to chat more as you move into the scaling phase!
