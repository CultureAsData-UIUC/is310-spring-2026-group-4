# Instructor Feedback on Initial Dataset Submission

Overall, this initial dataset is a really strong submission and the focus on single-serving meals and short-form cooking videos is well-scoped and clearly motivated. You do a great job of explaining why TikTok is an interesting site for studying the cultural framing of cooking for one, and the 60 manually annotated entries is a solid foundation that shows genuine close engagement with the material. The documentation is careful and reflective throughout, and I can tell you put real thought into the interpretive dimensions of the dataset rather than just going through the motions.

One small suggestion is that I would recommend converting the dataset fields section into a proper markdown table, which would make it much more legible. 

**1. What's Working Well**

The interpretative, methodological decisions are clearly thoughtful and well documented. Recording dish types descriptively rather than forcing them into a fixed controlled vocabulary is smart. It preserves the specificity of individual entries while still allowing for later categorization, as your `summarize_dataset.py` script demonstrates nicely. The three-level convenience framing (explicit / implicit / none) is well-defined and the documentation explains it clearly. I also appreciate that you didn't exclude carousel posts, treating them as a distinct format rather than a data quality problem shows good methodological instincts, and the distinction between timed videos and carousels is a genuinely interesting variable in itself.

Including URLs for each entry is good practice and I appreciate it. That said, as I mentioned in my earlier feedback, TikTok URLs are notoriously unstable, so I would strongly encourage you to download and locally archive the videos you're analyzing rather than relying on links alone. Content gets deleted, accounts get suspended, and you could lose access to your entire dataset.

One addition worth considering for the next phase: recording which search term or hashtag led you to each video directly in the dataset itself. Right now the documentation lists your search terms, which is good, but embedding that provenance at the entry level would make it much easier to analyze later whether different search terms surface different kinds of content, which might itself be an interesting finding.

I also appreciate the thoughtful design of `cli_data_entry.py`, using a command-line interface with confirmation prompts and structured field validation reduces the risk of inconsistencies creeping in across 60 entries. And `summarize_dataset.py` goes well beyond a basic summary: the parsing functions for likes (handling "30.8k" style formatting), duration (handling both seconds and "1min02s" style inputs), and the rule-based dish classification are all genuinely useful pieces of computational infrastructure. The decision to keep raw dish labels in the dataset while using the classification only for summary purposes is also exactly the right call. These scripts are the kind of computational work that augments interpretive labor rather than replacing it!

**2. Next Steps & Scaling Considerations**

My main concern going forward is the scaling plan, which is where I want to push you to think more carefully. You mention using hashtag-based scraping or APIs to collect 500–1,000 additional videos, but TikTok scraping is genuinely difficult. The platform actively resists it, and most scraping tools break regularly as TikTok updates its infrastructure. There is a Python library called `pyktok` [https://github.com/dfreelon/pyktok](https://github.com/dfreelon/pyktok) that you might explore, but I'd go in with realistic expectations about its reliability. It may be that you end up having to continue finding videos manually and leaning more heavily on computational assistance at the annotation stage rather than the collection stage, which is a legitimate approach, just worth planning for explicitly. That might mean for you that scaling the dataset is not so much in the number of videos but in the complexity of the annotations, something I am happy to allow though I would to briefly discuss to make sure it meets the assignment requirements (feel free to message me on Slack or book at chat via Calendly or chat with me after class!)

On the annotation side, I think your instinct toward NLP and LLMs for categorizing dish types and convenience narratives is strong potential approach. What I'd also encourage you to think about computer vision and whether object detection could be useful. Your `creator_presentation` variable in particular (hands only, face on camera, voiceover, text only) is exactly the kind of thing that computer vision could potentially assist with at scale, since it's fundamentally a visual distinction. To be clear, this is a suggestion and a fairly technically ambitious one, but given the sophistication of what you've already built, I think you're well-positioned to experiment with it and I would be happy to assist you with the code. It would also make for a genuinely interesting methodological contribution to your final data essay, reflecting on what gets gained and lost when you move from human annotation of video content to automated frame analysis.

The implicit convenience narrative is going to be the hardest thing to automate reliably, and you're right to flag it as a challenge. That's also what makes it the most interesting variable in your dataset. I'd think carefully about whether you want to try to automate it at scale or whether you keep it as a manually annotated field and use the scaling phase to expand the other, more automatable variables instead.
