**2. The Computational Work and Its Limitations**

The `annotation_helper.py` script is genuinely impressive and worth discussing in some detail. The system prompt design is clever. Framing Gemini as a "cultural food studies research assistant" is a thoughtful persona choice, and the coding definitions are precise enough to produce consistent structured output. I'm curious how you arrived at that prompt: was it something you refined iteratively after looking at early results? It might be worth experimenting with alternative personas to see whether a different framing produces meaningfully different annotations. That kind of prompt sensitivity analysis could be interesting methodological material for your final essay.

A few suggestions for the script itself. You might consider adding a rationale field to the JSON output, capturing how the model defends its assessments would give you useful contextual data and make verification easier. To help parse and validate structured responses from the model more robustly, you might look at the `Pydantic` [https://docs.pydantic.dev/latest/](https://docs.pydantic.dev/latest/), which is commonly used for exactly this kind of LLM output verification. It's also worth considering adding a field to your dataset that records the length of the text excerpt you provided to the model, since the number of tokens in the input can skew annotation results in ways that are worth tracking.

This connects directly to one of your key findings: the model's difficulty distinguishing between deliberate omission and structural absence is interesting, but it's also somewhat expected. Structural absence is hard to identify without contextual knowledge of what a source typically includes. One way to address this in future iterations would be to include in the prompt some assessment of source length relative to others in your dataset, something like flagging that a given source falls in the shortest quartile, which might help the model better distinguish omission from format constraint.

The `authenticity_framing` field might also worth revisiting at the prompt level given your findings. I suspect the model may be interpreting authenticity in terms of copyright or ownership rather than the cultural grounding you're actually after. It might be worth experimenting with slightly different language in that part of the prompt to see if you can get closer to what you mean (maybe substitute authenticity for originality perhaps?). Finally, though again this suggestion is more realistic if you continue with this project post-semester, you might experiment with multimodal LLMs and specifically, consider scraping and saving the full HTML page and passing it along with associated images to the model. Context window limitations make this tricky, but evaluating a source holistically rather than just from a text excerpt might help surface more interesting findings at the structural versus semantic layer. For the YouTube entries in particular, you could provide a selection of video frames to the model, or use object and color detection on shots to capture some of the visual dimensions you mention in the documentation.

I also want to flag something I'd love to see you develop in your final essay: you write that "Gemini returned a structured JSON annotation, which was then reviewed, evaluated, and either accepted or revised before being added to the dataset." That review process is doing a lot of important work, and making it visible––with a concrete example of a case where you disagreed with the model's output and why––would be one of the most interesting parts of your essay. 

**3. Patterns and Next Steps**

The patterns and questions section is strong, and the YouTube finding is particularly interesting. That community video channels behaving more like community blogs than recipe aggregators is a genuinely surprising result worth expanding on. I do wonder whether capturing more structural elements of sources––number of images, page length, layout features––might provide additional interesting context as you scale up. It would fit naturally with a scraping-based approach and could complement your existing qualitative coding.

For the scaling phase, I like the ambition of expanding to more dishes, though you might actually consider going deeper on the dishes you've already explored through adding more sources per dish, rather than broadening immediately. That would keep the dataset more interpretable and let you stress-test your coding framework more thoroughly before expanding scope. For web scraping, you might look at the Internet Archive Python library [https://archive.org/developers/internetarchive/](https://archive.org/developers/internetarchive/), which lets you access archived versions of websites saved as WARC files. The *Archives Unleashed Project* [https://archivesunleashed.org/](https://archivesunleashed.org/) has good examples of working with these computationally, and I'm happy to discuss how to use them if you want to go that route. Now that you've verified the LLM annotator on your bespoke dataset, using it at scale should work well, and if API costs or time limits become a concern, I'm also happy to discuss using open source models locally via Ollama as an alternative.

I think the Epicurious dataset idea is worth keeping as a backup option rather than a primary one. It would be interesting to compare their approach to yours, and I'd encourage you to look into the history of that dataset before committing to it. Understanding its provenance would fit well with your broader methodological commitments. For the harder interpretive fields like `community_credit_given` that are difficult to automate, you might try some simple text analysis (maybe TF-IDF or a regression model) to see if there are textual differences between sources you coded as giving community credit versus those that didn't. Structural features like source length, number of images, and source name might also be useful predictors, since I imagine a lot of the textual signals are dish- or community-specific. There might be something in verb choice in particular that indicates a source's orientation toward the immigrant community it's describing.

My overall suggestion for how to sequence the next phase: start by seeing what you can automate of the interpretive fields, then figure out what data you can actually collect at scale, and then assess how much of that data you can annotate reliably. That order will keep you from building a scraping pipeline before you know what it needs to produce.
