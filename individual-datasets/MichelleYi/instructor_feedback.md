# Instructor Feedback on Initial Dataset Submission

Overall, this is a solid submission and the focus on YouTube extreme eating content as digital performance is genuinely interesting. Narrowing your scope to three specific creators on one platform is a smart structural choice that gives you built-in axes for comparison without being overwhelming. The transparency about your computational tools is appreciated, and the acknowledgment that calorie estimation is inherently approximate makes sense. Since most food quantities in these videos are themselves exaggerated or imprecise, that struggle is actually part of the phenomenon you're studying rather than just a data quality problem.

Generally, the documentation would benefit from a dataset table showing each field with its data type and at least one concrete example of how it's been encoded. That would make the dataset much more legible to outside readers and is something worth adding before your final submission.

**1. The Dataset Needs More Interpretive Depth**

The most significant thing I want to push on is the analytical frame, and I want to be direct because I raised this in my earlier feedback as well. Right now the dataset is heavily focused on size and quantity––portion size, food quantity, estimated calories––and I'm not fully convinced that volume is the most interesting thing to capture here. Is your core question whether extreme eating videos contain similar amounts of food? That seems both unlikely to be true and perhaps missing the more culturally interesting phenomena. I'd really encourage you to think about what you might frame as genres of excess; not just how much food appears, but how it's presented, what kind of spectacle it creates, and how that changes across creators and over time. You can absolutely keep size as a variable, but the dataset currently lacks interpretive or subjective dimensions that would make it analytically richer.

One direction worth exploring is video and image composition. Rather than, or perhaps alongside of trying to quantify portion size through subjective judgment, you might consider using object detection to identify what is food versus person in the frame, and then use that more systematically to get at how excess is visually constructed. This would also help manage the consistency concerns you've flagged. I also noticed the documentation doesn't include any initial observations from working closely with the three creators you've already collected, so before expanding to more creators, it might be worth exploring what you already have to see whether broadening the sample will yield genuine new insight or just create noise through too many comparisons. What patterns are already visible within these three creators' content? How does the genre look different across them?

**2. Next Steps and Scaling**

The next steps plan has the right instincts but needs more specificity. For collecting additional videos, I'd recommend looking at `pytubefix` [https://pytubefix.readthedocs.io/en/latest/index.html](https://pytubefix.readthedocs.io/en/latest/index.html) for scraping, or the official YouTube Data API as a more stable alternative. If you do expand beyond your three current creators, make sure to record how you found each video (the search term or keyword that surfaced it) since that's a meaningful data point for understanding what the platform's algorithm considers "extreme eating" content and will help you account for any algorithmic skew in your sample.

That said, I'd encourage you to think carefully about whether expanding to more creators is actually the right move at this stage. You haven't yet fully explored what your existing dataset can tell you, and there's a real risk that broadening too quickly creates more comparability problems than it solves. One angle that might be more productive is tracking how these particular creators' content evolves over time: whether portions genuinely get larger, whether the composition of the videos shifts, whether the type of food changes. That longitudinal question within a focused set of creators could be more analytically interesting than a broader cross-creator comparison.

For the computational side, as I noted above, I'd strongly recommend experimenting with computer vision object detection, which is well-suited to distinguishing food from people in video frames and is trained on exactly the kind of visual sources you're working with. It might even help you get at food item identification in ways that could partially assist with calorie estimation. More importantly, it could help you track video composition over time––how the visual construction of excess changes as the genre evolves––which I think is a more interesting question than raw calorie counts. The idea of using an LLM for classification is also reasonable, and combining it with computer vision for the visual dimensions would give you a stronger methodological toolkit for the scaling phase.

Overall, the foundation is real and the topic has genuine cultural potential. The main thing holding this dataset back is that the current variables don't yet capture what's most interesting about extreme eating as a cultural form. Your final data essay will need to articulate a compelling rationale for why volume is the right lens, or pivot toward the richer compositional and performative dimensions that I think are actually at the heart of what makes this genre worth studying. Happy to chat more as you move into the scaling phase!
