**3. Reliability, Scaling, and Next Steps**

The data reliability and quality section is thoughtful and I largely agree with your assessment. One reframing I'd suggest: don't describe subjective coding as a limitation. That framing undersells what you're actually doing. Your interpretive judgments are the point, not a weakness to apologize for. What you might instead note is the consistency challenge that comes with being a sole researcher, though as you correctly observe, single-researcher datasets do have an inherent consistency that multi-coder projects sometimes struggle to achieve. The potential overlap in your cultural theme categories is worth flagging here as a reliability concern, since categories that blur into each other can make it harder to assess whether patterns are real or artifacts of how the coding scheme was structured.

On the next steps and scaling plan: the ambition here is impressive and the structure is strong, though I want to flag a few things worth adjusting. First, the TikTok Research API as I mentioned in-class is not going to be a viable route for this project. Access is extremely restricted and the approval process is lengthy and uncertain. I would recommend looking more seriously at PykTok for scraping, though it is far from perfect and will require some patience. Continuing with Apify is also possible, though costs can add up quickly. It might be worth digging into what Apify is actually doing under the hood; it's possible they're using something relatively straightforward like Python requests, in which case replicating their approach independently could save you money and give you more control. Given these constraints, I want to reassure you: if your scaled dataset doesn't hit 500 data points, that would be acceptable if you instead expand the richness and sophistication of your computational analysis. Quality of analysis can compensate for quantity of data, so keep that as a viable path.

For the automated classification work, I like the plan of using keywords combined with LLMs for categorization, though costs are a real factor to plan around. As an alternative to Claude or other paid APIs, I'd encourage you to look at open source models running locally via Ollama, something we'll be covering in class and it could significantly reduce your costs while giving you more flexibility. One advantage of using LLMs for classification is that you can also ask for a rationale alongside each classification decision, which would give you useful data for evaluating and refining your prompts and could also help surface some of the tensions and contradictions you've been tracking manually. That rationale field is worth building into your prompting strategy from the start.

I am more skeptical of the automated sentiment analysis approach using tools like VADER or TextBlob, and I want to be direct about why. These tools don't just fail to surface performative contradictions, they are trained on a fundamentally flattened and decontextualized view of language. VADER, for instance, will score "badass" as negative. For the kind of culturally situated, tonally nuanced content you're analyzing, that's a significant problem. I'd recommend replacing sentiment analysis with an expanded LLM classification approach that explicitly asks the model to assess tone as part of the same pipeline you're using for thematic categorization. LLM outputs have their own instabilities and shouldn't be treated as ground truth, but they at least treat language as contextual in ways that VADER and TextBlob don't. You might also look at the `shorttext` Python library [https://shorttext.readthedocs.io/en/latest/](https://shorttext.readthedocs.io/en/latest/) for doing classification based on textual features as running a logistic regression or Naive Bayes model on caption patterns could give you a useful third perspective alongside your keyword rules and LLM classifications. In fact, a particularly strong methodological contribution would be comparing all three approaches (regex and keyword counting, a regression or BERT model, and LLM classification) on your initial 49-video dataset as a case study, and seeing where they converge and where they diverge. That kind of methodological triangulation would make for genuinely interesting material in your final essay, and I'd be happy to help you implement that code if you want to try it out.

For the moralizing language detection specifically, I think a workflow that starts with regex and keyword counting and then compares those results to model-based classifications is exactly right. Capturing caption length as a field would also be worth adding, both to contextualize whatever patterns emerge and because length may itself be a meaningful variable. Longer captions might correlate with different kinds of wellness framing than shorter ones.

On the computer vision question: I appreciate your awareness of how difficult full video analysis would be, and I think the right call for now is to treat captions and hashtags as your primary object of study rather than the videos themselves. A potential middle ground for future iterations would be selecting a small number of representative frames per video (maybe as few as three shots each) and running object detection on those static images rather than the full video. That approach would likely work well for detecting food versus person in the frame and could even assist with some of your more visual categorization questions. But that's genuinely a lot for one project, and I'd save it for post-semester rather than building it into your current scaling plan.

The research questions at scale are strong, though I'd encourage you to focus on one or two rather than trying to pursue all of them simultaneously. The scope is ambitious enough that narrowing will serve you better than breadth. On ethics and anonymization: your instinct to anonymize creators and only directly quote those with large public followings is the right one, and I'd encourage you to return to our course readings on TikTok and user privacy to help you situate and justify those choices in your final essay. The University of Michigan's Social Media Archive [socialmediaarchive.org](socialmediaarchive.org) is also worth checking for any previously scraped datasets that might overlap with your content area and save you some collection work.

Finally, I want to say that your idea of using categorization errors as a signal for revising your LLM prompting strategy is really smart. That kind of iterative, self-correcting approach to prompt engineering is methodologically sophisticated. And more broadly, your instinct to treat the process itself as a form of data (tracking what gets missed, what gets miscategorized, and what that reveals about the limits of automation) is exactly the right way to think about this kind of project. One request for your final submission: please include all scripts. The data processing code is an important part of the documentary record and needs to be there for the work to be fully reproducible.

Overall, I am very impressed with your initial results and genuinely excited to see where it goes. The cultural framing is sharp, the methodological self-awareness is sophisticated, and the patterns you've surfaced from 49 videos already suggest a rich analysis ahead. My main suggestion as you move into the scaling phase is to focus your computational efforts (potentially starting with captions and hashtags as your primary unit of analysis) rather than trying to pursue every dimension simultaneously. That focus will make the project more manageable without sacrificing what's most analytically interesting about it. Happy to discuss more at any point!
