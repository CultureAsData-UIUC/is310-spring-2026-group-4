# Instructor Feedback on Initial Dataset Submission

Overall, I can tell you are approaching this topic carefully and with genuine concern for the communities you're trying to study, and that care comes through in your documentation. The focus on how platform algorithms may disadvantage certain creators and cuisines is a genuinely interesting and important question, and I appreciate the thoughtfulness behind choices like using a fresh TikTok account to simulate an unbiased starting point. That said, I have some significant concerns about the current direction that I want to address directly and honestly, because I think the project needs some meaningful recalibration before it can move forward responsibly.

**1. The Racial Categorization Needs to Be Removed**

I want to start here because it's the most pressing issue. I raised concerns about coding perceived race and ethnicity in my earlier feedback, but I want to be more direct now that I've seen the dataset: I'm asking you to remove the racial identity field from your final dataset entirely. I don't usually share this with students, but I am mixed race, and I will tell you that in the entirety of my life no one has ever categorized me accurately based on visual perception alone. What happens if one of these creators finds your dataset and your classification of their identity is wrong? Or even if it's right but they never consented to being categorized that way? These are real, identifiable people attached to real TikTok accounts and video links, and imposing racial labels on them based on visual cues creates genuine ethical exposure, regardless of how carefully it's done.

I can see that you already feel this discomfort yourself. You describe your approach as a "band-aid solution" and say it "felt wrong to impose a label." I think that instinct is correct and worth trusting. The good news is that I don't think this field is actually necessary for the question you're trying to answer, and its removal will make the dataset stronger rather than weaker. That said, the process of discovering these limitations, working through what it means to try to categorize identity from visual cues, and why that breaks down would make a genuinely interesting and reflective section of your final data essay. The methodological struggle is worth documenting even if the field itself doesn't belong in the dataset.

**2. The Core Questions Need Significant Rethinking**

Related to this, I want to flag a few broader conceptual issues with the current framing. First, I'm concerned that the project is projecting American racial categories onto a global platform without enough critical distance. TikTok operates across many cultural contexts, and the racial politics that shape visibility and bias in the United States don't map cleanly onto a global user base. Approaching this intersectionally and with more geographic specificity would significantly strengthen the analysis.

Second, and this is important: by selecting only videos with over 100k likes, you are specifically choosing content that has not been disadvantaged by the algorithm. If your goal is to study algorithmic bias against certain creators or cuisines, selecting for viral content works against that goal. You're looking at what succeeded, not what was suppressed. That's a fundamental tension in the current methodology that needs to be resolved.

I'd encourage you to narrow your focus considerably. Is there a specific place, community, or type of cuisine that genuinely interests you? Starting there would allow you to ask more precise questions. For instance, if you focused on food content from a specific city or region (maybe Champaign-Urbana or Chicago ), you could start to ask who gets more engagement and why. Though even then you'd need to account carefully for the difference between algorithmic bias and other factors like content quality or creator experience. TikTok's algorithm is also specifically interest-based rather than purely follower- or view-weighted, which means bias operates differently there than on other platforms, and that's worth engaging with directly.

One more promising angle, which I mentioned in my earlier feedback and want to return to: rather than trying to prove that certain cuisines are discriminated against, you might look at when and how the term "ethnic" gets deployed in food content on TikTok. Is "ethnic food" even a permitted tag on the platform? I notice none of the videos you collected use it. How do racialized terms like "ethnic" circulate or get suppressed on a platform like TikTok? That's a more tractable and frankly more interesting question than trying to measure algorithmic bias through visual identity coding, and it would let you approach the politics of food categorization critically rather than reproducing its assumptions.

I'm also skeptical of the food origin field as currently conceived, though I noted it is not in your current dataset so it is difficult ot assess. You're already discovering in your challenges section how contested and difficult it is to assign cultural origins to food (Chipotle being a good example) and I think that difficulty is telling you something important. Either this field needs to be significantly rethought or dropped, because as you've found, the categories don't hold up under scrutiny.
